and timelike Compton scattering (TCS) leading twist amplitudes are intimately related thanks to their analytic properties as a function of Q 2. While many experimental data have already been reported for DVCS <ref type="bibr" target="#b0">[1,</ref><ref type="bibr" target="#b1">2]</ref>, TCS experimental studies did only very recently report their first results <ref type="bibr" target="#b2">[3]</ref>. The aim of our study <ref type="bibr" target="#b3">[4]</ref> is to quantify the complementarity of DVCS and TCS data to extract information on generalized parton distributions (GPDs), in as much as possible a model-independent way, helped by artificial neutral network techniques.</p></div>\n<div><head n="2">Relation between DVCS and TCS amplitudes</head><p>The straightforward intimate connection between DVCS and TCS amplitudes was noticed in the early papers <ref type="bibr" target="#b4">[5,</ref><ref type="bibr" target="#b5">6]</ref> and made explicit at the next to leading order (NLO) in \u03b1 s in <ref type="bibr" target="#b6">[7,</ref><ref type="bibr" target="#b7">8]</ref>. After a decomposition of scattering amplitudes in products of Dirac structures and Compton form factors (CFF), factorization theorems allow to express these CFFs in terms of perturbatively calculable coefficient functions T i and GPDs F i , where i denotes the various parton types:</p><formula id="formula_2">F(\u03be, t, Q 2 ) = 1 \u22121 d x i=u,d,...,g T i (x, \u03be, Q 2 )F i (x, \u03be, t) .<label>(3)</label></formula><p>The simple spacelike-to-timelike relations derived in Ref. <ref type="bibr" target="#b8">[9]</ref> allow us to express the NLO timelike coefficients by the spacelike ones as:</p><formula id="formula_3">T T i NLO = \xb1 S T i * \u2213 i\u03c0 \u03b1 s (\xb5 2 R ) 2\u03c0 S C i * coll ,<label>(4)</label></formula><p>where left superscripts S and T respectively denote spacelike and timelike quantities, and upper (lower) sign is for (anti-)symmetric coefficient functions in \u03be; S C i coll can be found in Ref. <ref type="bibr" target="#b8">[9]</ref>. For (anti-)symmetric CFFs H ( H) this gives:</p><formula id="formula_4">T H NLO = + S H * \u2212 i\u03c0 Q 2 \u2202 \u2202 Q 2 S H * , T H NLO = \u2212 S H * + i\u03c0 Q 2 \u2202 \u2202 Q 2 S H * ,<label>(5)</label></formula><p>and similar relations for (anti-)symmetric CFFs E ( E). The bands show 68% confidence level for the uncertainties of presented quantities. The dashed (solid) lines correspond to the GK GPD model <ref type="bibr" target="#b10">[11]</ref><ref type="bibr" target="#b11">[12]</ref><ref type="bibr" target="#b12">[13]</ref> evaluated with LO (NLO) TCS coefficient functions.</p><p>DVCS measurements off a proton target. This technique is known to lead to an essential reduction of model dependency. In our analysis the replica method was used to propagate experimental uncertainties to those of extracted quantities. Let us now present some illustrative results (see <ref type="bibr" target="#b3">[4]</ref> for more plots). In <ref type="figure" target="#fig_0">Fig. 1</ref> we show the extracted DVCS CFF S H (shaded gray band) as a function of \u03be for exemplary kinematics of Q 2 = 2 GeV 2 , t = \u22120.3 GeV 2 . For comparison, we also present a model prediction based on the Goloskokov-Kroll (GK) parametrization of GPDs <ref type="bibr" target="#b10">[11]</ref><ref type="bibr" target="#b11">[12]</ref><ref type="bibr" target="#b12">[13]</ref>, obtained with LO (dashed line) and NLO (solid line) coefficient functions.  <ref type="figure" target="#fig_1">Fig.2</ref> shows the \u03be-dependence of the dominant TCS, CFF \u03be T H(\u03be) for Q 2 = 2 GeV 2 and t = \u22120.3 GeV 2 using both LO and NLO relations between the DVCS and TCS amplitudes. The bigger uncertainty attached to the NLO case is representative of the very bad knowledge of the Q 2 dependence of DVCS amplitudes, reflecting the sparsity and limited range in Q 2 of the used data. Turning the argument around, this indicates that a moderately precise TCS measurement would help much in quantifying this Q 2 dependence which is of utmost importance to fully understand the QCD dynamics of these processes. The denominator of this asymmetry is dominated by the square of BH amplitude, which is almost flat in \u03c6.</p></div>\n<div><head n="4">Conclusion</head><p>Our data-driven study of TCS has demonstrated how much it is important to use both DVCS and TCS data to access in a sensible way the GPDs of the nucleon. It also showed in a quantitative way why any extraction of GPDs based on a leading order analysis of experimental data is very incomplete and model-dependent. In particular, the analytical (in Q 2 ) relation between NLO coefficient functions of DVCS and TCS results in a quite unique way to access the Q 2 dependence of the GPDs. Other reactions should be analyzed in the same way.
